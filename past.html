Physics Research

I have far-ranging interests within quantum condensed matter theory and as part of my Ph.D. thesis worked on many-body localization (3), topological phases (3, 4), non-Fermi liquids (2), and topologically protected mechanical systems (1).

Summary of Publications

1. Hoi Chun Po, Yasaman Bahri, and Ashvin Vishwanath. “Phonon analog of topological nodal semimetals.” Phys. Rev. B. 93, 205158 (2016). Editor's Suggestion

  • We presented a construction of the first topologically protected mechanical system that is gapless in its bulk.

2. Yasaman Bahri and Andrew C. Potter. “Stable non-Fermi-liquid phase of itinerant spin-orbit coupled ferromagnets.” Phys. Rev. B 92, 035131 (2015).

  • We discussed a simple construction of a metal coupled to Goldstone bosons that, unlike most other cases, gives rise to a non-Fermi rather than a Fermi liquid. The ingredients are a ferromagnetic metal with broken continuous rotation symmetry and spin-orbit coupling. We showed within a combined N, epsilon expansion that the non-Fermi liquid is expected to be robust to the relevant instabilities (superconducting and spin-density wave orders). This gave one of the few known examples of a phase of coupled fermions and bosons where the non-Fermi liquid would not be masked at low temperatures.

3. Yasaman Bahri, Ronen Vosk, Ehud Altman, and Ashvin Vishwanath. “Localization and topology protected quantum coherence at the edge of hot matter.” Nature Communications 6:7341 (2015).

  • We showed for the first time that many-body localization and symmetry-protected topological order together can give rise to quantum coherent dynamics at the edge of a many-body system.

4. Yasaman Bahri and Ashvin Vishwanath. “Detecting Majorana fermions in quasi-one-dimensional topological phases using nonlocal order parameters.” Phys. Rev. B 89, 155135 (2014).

  • We devised nonlocal order parameters (measurements requiring an extensive number of operators) for certain classes of low-dimensional symmetry-protected topological phases of fermions. The motivation was to have order parameters that are simple and potentially measurable with new experimental probes, e.g. a quantum gas microscope.


In my undergraduate thesis research, I worked on a scaling theory for 1D quantum spin systems near a quantum critical point that combined both the effects of finite size (“finite-size scaling”) and finite entanglement (“finite-entanglement scaling”), where the latter is induced due to the limitations of representation by tensors networks.
